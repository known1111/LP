Query 7 ‚Äî ‚ÄúDisplay the amount of those accounts whose amount is higher than amount of any saving account amount.‚Äù
This is ambiguous in plain English. Two interpretations:


Interpretation A (higher than any saving account) means Amount > ANY (saving amounts) ‚Äî that is satisfied if the account amount is greater than at least one saving-account amount (which is true for most accounts).


Interpretation B (higher than all saving accounts) means Amount > ALL (saving amounts) ‚Äî i.e., higher than every saving amount (strictly greater than the maximum saving).


I‚Äôll give both queries and their results.
-- A) > ANY (greater than at least one saving account)
SELECT Acc_id, C_id, Acc_Type, Amount
FROM Account
WHERE Amount > ANY (
  SELECT Amount FROM Account WHERE Acc_Type = 'Saving'
);

-- B) > ALL (greater than all saving accounts i.e., greater than the max saving)
SELECT Acc_id, C_id, Acc_Type, Amount
FROM Account
WHERE Amount > ALL (
  SELECT Amount FROM Account WHERE Acc_Type = 'Saving'
);

From the dataset saving amounts: [50000,30000,75000,35000,80000,10000], maximum saving = 80000.


Result A (> ANY): returns ALL accounts whose amount > at least one saving amount. That is every account except those that are ‚â§ every saving amount (practically almost all). Concretely, amounts 20000,30000,... Most rows satisfy because at least one saving (10000) is small. So result A returns rows with amounts: 50000,20000,30000,75000,35000,50000,90000,80000,45000 (i.e., all except the saving 10000 itself if it is NOT greater than any saving? 10000 > ANY(saving)? 10000 > ANY? No because ANY contains 10000 itself; 10000 > ANY(saving amounts) is false). So everything except Acc 9 shows up.


Result B (> ALL): amounts strictly greater than 80000 (max saving) ‚Üí only amount 90000 (Acc 7).
